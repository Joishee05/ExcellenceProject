Text Formatting

Lesson 4 of 15

Text formatting allows you to emphasize and highlight different parts of your content. HTML provides several tags for this purpose, and it's important to understand the difference between semantic and presentational formatting.

Semantic vs Presentational Formatting

Before diving into specific tags, let's understand an important distinction:

Semantic Formatting

What it means: The tag describes the meaning of the content to browsers and screen readers

<strong> and <em>

Benefits:

  • Better accessibility for screen readers
  • Better for SEO (search engines understand importance)
  • Cleaner, more standards-compliant HTML

Presentational Formatting

What it means: The tag only tells the browser how to display the text visually

<b> and <i>

Limitations:

  • Doesn't convey meaning to assistive technologies
  • Less valuable for SEO
  • Relies purely on visual styling

Strong Tag (<strong>)

The <strong> tag indicates that the text has strong importance. Browsers typically render it as bold text. Screen readers emphasize the word differently.

When to Use:

  • Important warnings or alerts
  • Critical information the reader must know
  • Key terms being defined
  • Emphasis for understanding

Strong Tag Example:

<p>Warning: Do not touch the electrical outlet.</p>
<p>The deadline is Friday at 5 PM – no extensions.</p>

Emphasis Tag (<em>)

The <em> tag indicates emphasis. Browsers typically render it as italic text. Use this to emphasize words or phrases that change the meaning of a sentence.

When to Use:

  • Emphasizing important words
  • Changing sentence meaning through stress
  • Foreign words or technical terms
  • Book or movie titles

Emphasis Tag Example:

<p>I really enjoyed that movie.</p>
<p>The word context is important in programming.</p>
<p>I'm reading The Great Gatsby this month.</p>

Bold Tag (<b>) - Use With Caution

The <b> tag makes text bold without implying importance. It's purely presentational. Use sparingly and only when <strong> isn't appropriate.

When to Use:

  • Product names in product listings
  • First sentence of article summaries
  • Keywords in search results
  • When you need bold text but not semantic importance

Bold Tag Example:

<p>Latest News: New iPhone Released</p>
<p>The product SmartWatch Pro is now available.</p>

Italic Tag (<i>) - Use With Caution

The <i> tag makes text italic without implying emphasis. Like <b>, it's purely presentational. Prefer <em> in most cases.

When to Use:

  • Alternative voice or tone (like a character's thoughts)
  • Musical or artistic titles
  • Foreign words that need to be styled differently
  • Technical terms in specific contexts

Italic Tag Example:

<p>She thought to herself, This is never going to work.</p>
<p>The theme of this painting is solitude.</p>

Other Formatting Tags

<mark>

Highlights text like a highlighter pen

Use for: Search results highlighting, important passages

<small>

Makes text smaller than normal

Use for: Fine print, disclaimers, copyright

<del>

Shows deleted or removed text

Use for: Showing revisions, corrections

<ins>

Shows inserted or added text

Use for: Showing additions, corrections

Formatting Best Practices

✓ DO
  • Use <strong> for important content
  • Use <em> for emphasis
  • Keep formatting minimal and purposeful
  • Use CSS for pure styling needs
  • Combine semantic tags with CSS styling
✗ DON'T
  • Use <b> when <strong> fits better
  • Use <i> when <em> is more appropriate
  • Over-format text (avoid excessive emphasis)
  • Use formatting for accessibility issues
  • Ignore semantic meaning of tags

Real-World Example

Here's how you might format a product review or article with proper text formatting:

<article>
  <h1>Product Review: SmartWatch Pro</h1>
  <p>The SmartWatch Pro is an excellent choice for fitness enthusiasts. I was really impressed by its battery life.</p>
  <p>Important: This device is not waterproof. Keep it away from water.</p>
  <p>Price: $299 (previously $399)</p>
</article>

Why Text Formatting Matters

Understanding semantic vs presentational formatting is crucial because: It affects how assistive technologies (screen readers) interpret your content, influences SEO rankings, makes your HTML more maintainable, and ensures your content is accessible to all users. Good text formatting practices show you understand web standards and best practices.
Key Takeaways
  • <strong> conveys importance; <em> conveys emphasis
  • <b> and <i> are presentational, not semantic
  • Semantic formatting helps accessibility and SEO
  • Prefer semantic tags (<strong>, <em>) over presentational ones
  • <mark> highlights important text like a highlighter pen
  • <del> and <ins> show changes and revisions
  • Less formatting is usually better than excessive formatting
  • Use CSS for complex styling needs, not HTML formatting tags
